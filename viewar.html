AC4 Office Chair by Vitra

The aim behind the structural design of AC 4 was to discreetly integrate the functional elements required for optimal comfort. Up until now, the design of office chairs has emphasised either their aesthetic appearance or their functional features. By contrast, the integration of ergonomic features in AC 4 is so subtle that its sophisticated structural components remain concealed. It achieves a balance between the most advanced ergonomic functions and an unobtrusive appearance: the innovative technical features of AC 4 are neither put on display, nor are they hidden beneath foam padding. AC 4 is an emblematic object with an elegant appearance and uncompromising functionality, ideally suited for refined offices and conference spaces.

Armchair Rocker by Vitra Eames

The Rocking Armchair Rod Base, or RAR, is a small rocking chair mounted on wooden pads. Its iconic shape adds a remarkable accent to any environment. 'Making the best accessible to as many people as possible' was how Charles and Ray Eames described one of their overriding goals as furniture designers. Among their creations, no other allowed them to approach as much of this ideal as the Plastic Chairs. The idea of creating a one-piece seat shell, adapted to human anatomy, preoccupied the design couple for many years.

Farnsworth House by Lego

Few one-room homes are as strikingly modern and instantly recognizable as the Farnsworth House™, designed by Ludwig Mies van der Rohe. This single-story steel structure with floor-to-ceiling glass walls was meant to open a minimalist interior to nature in an extreme way. Construction took place from 1945–1951 on a 60-acre estate beside the Fox River in Plano, Illinois, where it still stands today. The assembled Farnsworth House model is over 11.9” (30.4cm) wide on a base with printed name label and includes a booklet with facts about the building, its construction and history.

Infinity Clock by Bosa Ceramiche

Infinity clock was designed by Nika Zupanc for Bosa Ceramiche and first presented during Salone del Mobile 2014 at Museum Bagatti – Valsecchi in Milan. Each pointer carries a loop and when they both meet things become charmingly complicated. The pointers and their loops form an intriguing visual structure that in the next moment already disintegrates before our own eyes. Infinity Clock has a soul of its own, even if just an artificial one – when it senses our approach it starts to move gently. Its swinging action reminds us that this moment is the only one that is real and the sole one worth living for.
